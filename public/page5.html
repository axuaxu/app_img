<h2 id="flickity-v2-released-groupcells-adaptiveheight-parallax">Flickity v2 released: groupCells, adaptiveHeight, parallax</h2>

<p><a href="http://flickity.metafizzy.co">Flickity is the best carousel library there is</a>. Since its initial release last year, Flickity has grown to be hugely popular (thank you for making that happen!), being put to use in high-profile sites like <a href="https://www.engadget.com/2016/01/05/canon-new-powershot-vixia-cameras/">Engadget</a>, <a href="https://www.artsy.net/show/henrique-faria-fine-art-full-emptiness-slash-el-vacio-lleno?from-show-guide=true">Artsy</a>, and <a href="https://yeezysupply.com/products/season-2-military-crepe-boot-women">Kanye West&apos;s yeezysupply.com</a>. With version 2, Flickity cements its status as the go-to carousel.</p>
<p>Version 2 is a huge upgrade. Let&apos;s take a look at some of its new features.</p>
<p>Groups cells together to act as individual slides with <a href="http://flickity.metafizzy.co/options.html#groupcells"><code>groupCells</code></a>.</p>

<h2 id="new-tech-gets-chatter">New tech gets chatter</h2>

<p>My text editor of choice, <a href="https://macromates.com/">TextMate</a>, is old. Version 1 was released in 2004. Version 2 was announced in 2009 and has been in alpha &amp; beta release since 2012. TextMate is old enough that a whole new generation of text editors have emerged and eclipsed it, most notably <a href="https://www.sublimetext.com/">Sublime Text</a> and <a href="https://atom.io/">Atom</a>. They have built upon TextMate&apos;s successes and learned from its pain points. But I continue to use TextMate, and will continue to TextMate. Because TextMate works for me.</p>
<p>You don&apos;t hear about TextMate because TextMate is old. What would I tweet? <em>Still using TextMate. Still good.</em> TextMate&apos;s problems are well known. Its hacky solutions are documented. Most everything&apos;s been covered.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Atom is entirely new. Its problems are new problems. When you find a solution, you might be the first one to discover it. That&apos;s pretty special. That&apos;s worth tweeting.</p>
<p>Seeing all the talk around Atom chips away at the confidence I have in TextMate. I feel like a stubborn luddite, clinging on to my 2005 flip-phone because I can see the numbers better. But that feeling, of missing-out because others are talking about it, is just a perceived social pressure, a herd behavior.</p>
<p>As a tech worker, I would like to think I am a highly rational being of pure logic. But I am just as susceptible to emotions as the rest of the muggles. So I recognize and tune-out hubbub and go with what I know. Because it continues to work, even though continuing to work isn&apos;t worthy of mention.</p>
<p>I use <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2012/7/20/3172222/google-buys-sparrow-mail">Sparrow</a> for email. I write CSS in vanilla CSS. I write JavaScript in vanilla JavaScript (ES5 at that, kids). I haven&apos;t written a line of React.</p>
<p>If you use React and you like it: great. But if you don&apos;t use React (or whatever hot new tech), and you feel like you should: don&apos;t worry about it. You are perfectly okay to stick with what works for you. The more you use something, the clearer its pain points become. Try new technologies when you&apos;re ready to address those pain points. Don&apos;t feel obligated to change your workflow because of chatter. New tech gets chatter, but that doesn&apos;t make it any better.</p>
<p>In this very year of 2016, George R. R. Martin is writing the most popular work of fiction of this century <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/future_tense/2014/05/14/george_r_r_martin_writes_on_dos_based_wordstar_4_0_software_from_the_1980s.html">on a 1980&apos;s DOS machine</a> running WordStar 4.0. <a href="http://grrm.livejournal.com/">He blogs on LiveJournal</a>.</p>
